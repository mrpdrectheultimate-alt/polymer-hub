## What is a Polymer?

A **polymer** is a large molecule (macromolecule) made up of many repeating smaller units called **monomers**, joined together by covalent bonds. The word comes from Greek: *poly* (many) + *meros* (parts).

Think of a polymer chain like a train — the engine and coaches are all connected, and the length of the train determines how strong and heavy it is.

**Examples you see every day:**
- Polypropylene (PP) — woven sacks, chairs, pipes
- Polyethylene (PE) — carry bags, water tanks
- PVC — water supply pipes, window frames
- Nylon — gears, ropes, textile fibres

---

## Degree of Polymerization (DP)

The **Degree of Polymerization** is the number of monomer units in a single polymer chain.

```
DP = Molecular Weight of Polymer / Molecular Weight of Monomer
```

**Example:** If PVC has a molecular weight of 100,000 g/mol and vinyl chloride monomer has a molecular weight of 62.5 g/mol:

```
DP = 100,000 / 62.5 = 1,600 repeating units
```

Higher DP → longer chains → stronger, tougher material.

---

## Types of Molecular Weight

Polymers are not a single chain length — they are a **distribution** of chains of varying lengths. We describe this with:

- **Number Average Molecular Weight (Mn):** Average based on number of chains. Sensitive to small molecules.
- **Weight Average Molecular Weight (Mw):** Average based on weight contribution. Sensitive to large chains.
- **Polydispersity Index (PDI) = Mw / Mn**

| PDI Value | Meaning |
|-----------|--------|
| PDI = 1.0 | Perfect uniformity (theoretical) |
| PDI 1.5–2.0 | Typical commercial polymers |
| PDI > 3.0 | Broad distribution, variable properties |

**Rule:** Lower PDI = more uniform properties = better processing consistency.

---

## How Molecular Weight Affects Properties

| Property | Low MW | High MW |
|----------|--------|---------|
| Tensile Strength | Low | High |
| Melt Viscosity | Low (easy flow) | High (difficult flow) |
| Toughness | Brittle | Tough |
| Processing | Easy | Difficult |
| Chemical Resistance | Low | High |

This is why **pipe-grade HDPE** (needs strength) has higher MW than **film-grade HDPE** (needs easy flow for thin films).

---

## Chain Architecture

Polymer chains are not always straight. Their architecture determines properties:

- **Linear:** Chains with no branches → high density, strong (HDPE)
- **Branched:** Side chains on backbone → lower density, more flexible (LDPE)
- **Cross-linked:** Chains joined by bridges → thermosets, rubber vulcanization
- **Star-shaped / Dendritic:** Used in specialty applications

### Indian Industry Example

**Reliance Industries Limited (RIL)** is India's largest polymer producer. Their **Relene HDPE** grades are tailored by molecular weight:

- **Relene 45084** — pipe grade (high MW, high strength, DP ~5,000)
- **Relene 45009** — injection moulding grade (lower MW, easy flow)
- **Relene films** — blown film grade (controlled MW distribution)

RIL's Hazira and Dahej petrochemical complexes in Gujarat produce over 2 million tonnes of polyolefins annually. The MW distribution is controlled during polymerization using **Ziegler-Natta or metallocene catalysts** — giving precise PDI control for each application grade.

CIPET (Central Institute of Petrochemicals Engineering & Technology) trains students to test MW using **Gel Permeation Chromatography (GPC)** — the standard method in Indian polymer labs.

---

## Key Takeaways

- A polymer is made of repeating monomer units; chain length is measured as Degree of Polymerization (DP)
- Molecular weight distribution (PDI) determines property consistency — lower PDI means more uniform material
- Higher molecular weight = stronger, tougher, harder to process; lower MW = weaker but easier to mould
